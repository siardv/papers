Univariate outliers

The boxplots presented provide methods for detecting univariate outliers in the distributions of daily caloric intake and the Dutch Healthy Diet (DHD) index. This technique defines the Interquartile Range (IQR) as \(Q_3 - Q_1\), where \(Q_3\) is the 75th percentile, and \(Q_1\) is the 25th percentile (Tukey, 1977). Values below \(Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) or above \(Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\) were considered outliers. The boxplot for caloric intake demonstrated the precision of the IQR method in identifying outliers, as indicated by the numerous outlier points (\(n = 46\)) with very high values. This clearly showed that individuals with extremely high caloric intake exceeded the boundaries set by the middle data range. In contrast, the MAD approach, which defines outliers as points outside the median \(\pm 2.5 \times \text{MAD}\) and calculates the MAD as the median of the absolute deviations from the median, fits more clustered DHD index scores (Leys et al., 2013). The DHD index boxplot shows the robustness of the MAD approach in detecting outliers. A few outliers (\(n = 11\)) suggested that diet adherence scores clustered more tightly around the mean. In summary, each variable plot visualizes outlier identification using appropriate statistical techniques for that distribution, highlighting how the IQR method suits the wider spread and outliers of the caloric intake data. By contrast, the MAD approach demonstrated reliability for more clustered data.

References: Leys, C., Ley, C., Klein, O., Bernard, P., & Licata, L. (2013). Detecting outliers: Do not use standard deviation around the mean, use absolute deviation around the median. Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 49(4), 764-766. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jesp.2013.03.013

Tukey, J. W. (1977). Exploratory data analysis. Addison-Wesley.

To create a composite socioeconomic position (SEP index, we first need to assess the reliability of the individual SEP measures. We will then derive the optimal weights for each SEP measure to predict district health disparities. Finally, we will calculate the SEP index using the optimal weights.

Reliability of SEP Measures

The reliability of socioeconomic position (SEP) measures is crucial for constructing a valid composite index. We will assess the reliability of the individual SEP measures using the Spearman-Brown coefficient, which is recommended for two-item scales due to its accuracy and unbiasedness compared to other reliability coefficients (Eisinga et al., 2012).

As shown by Eisinga et al. (2012), the Spearman-Brown coefficient is the most appropriate reliability coefficient for a two-item scale. Other popular measures, such as Cronbach’s alpha and Pearson’s correlation coefficient, are less accurate for two-item scales, yielding underestimates of reliability.

The Spearman-Brown coefficient converts the inter-item correlation into a reliability coefficient with the same interpretation as coefficient alpha. It assumes that two items are tau-equivalent measures of the same underlying construct. The formula is:

\(\rho_{SB} = \frac{2r_{xy}}{1+r_{xy}}\)

where \(r_{xy}\) is the Pearson correlation between the items \(x\) and \(y\).

The Spearman-Brown formula represents the relationship between the proportion of variance explained by the true score (reliability) and that unexplained for each item. As the inter-item correlation increases, the Spearman-Brown coefficient provides a progressively more precise and unbiased estimate of reliability compared to the coefficient alpha based on simulations. For two congeneric items, the Spearman-Brown statistic has a lower bias on average than the coefficient alpha across the full range of possible inter-item correlations. The Spearman-Brown coefficient ranged from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating greater internal consistency reliability.

Optimal Weights for SEP Measures

The common practice of equally weighting education and occupation when constructing socioeconomic position (SEP) indices has significant limitations and risks misrepresenting the true social stratification. Rather than assuming equal contributions, a methodologically rigorous approach empirically derives weights to reflect the components’ actual impacts on each population (Adler & Stewart, 2010). Data-driven weighting creates a precise SEP index that maximizes validity and enhances the analysis of disparities.

A substantial body of evidence demonstrates that the influences of education versus occupation differ substantially across cohorts and genders due to shifting societal expectations and opportunities over the decades (Goyette, 2008; Taniguchi & Kaufman, 2005). For instance, educational attainment became increasingly important for younger generations as postsecondary access expanded dramatically in the late 20th century. Consequently, occupational status is more impactful for older cohorts who enter the workforce when college is rare (Fischer & Hout, 2006).

Similarly, due to historical workforce exclusions, occupational status served as a stronger SEP marker for men, while education was more salient for women (Torssander & Erikson, 2010). Although these gendered patterns have evolved, occupational segregation between men and women persists and contributes to uneven SEP processes between groups (Tomaskovic-Devey et al., 2021).

Empirically estimating weights using regression models allows for the construction of tailored SEP indices for each population subgroup (Krieger et al., 1997), in this case each family member (respondent, partner, father, mother). In contrast, applying assumed equal weights risks substantially misestimating individual SEP and obscuring the true nature of social stratification, which has significant implications for health disparities research and policy recommendations (Krieger et al., 1997).

The optimal weights for education and occupation are derived through regression models minimizing root mean squared error (RMSE) between predicted and observed district health disparity indices. The RMSE is a widely used measure of model fit, quantifying the average prediction error. Lower RMSE values indicate better model fit, with the optimal weights maximizing the composite SEP index’s linkage to disparities. Although RMSE has limitations (i.e., sensitivity to outliers), it is preferred over other fit indices for regression models due to its interpretability and practical meaning.

The Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) is often preferred over other fit indices for regression models due to its interpretability and because it shares the same units as the outcome variable. Specifically, RMSE represents the standard deviation of the residuals (prediction errors), quantifying on average how far off the predictions \(\widehat{y}_i\) are from the true outcomes \(y_i\). Mathematically, RMSE is defined as \(\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \widehat{y}_i)^2}\) where \(n\) is the number of observations. A lower RMSE indicates less variation around the regression line, suggesting that the model fits the data better. Compared to \(R^2\), RMSE is more sensitive to outliers, thus avoiding overinflated performance metrics. Overall, RMSE strikes a balance between interpretability, sensitivity, and practical significance.

optimal_weights <- function(columns, dataset) {
  if (missing(dataset)) {
    optimal_weights_data <- getOption("optimal_weights_data")
    if (!is.null(optimal_weights_data)) {
      dataset <- get(optimal_weights_data, envir = .GlobalEnv)
    }
  }
  df <- data.frame(sapply(dataset[columns], as.numeric))
  w_education <- seq(0.3, 0.7, by = 0.001)
  w_occupation <- 1 - w_education
  w_df <- data.frame(w_education = NA, w_occupation = NA, rmse = NA)
  for (i in seq_along(w_education)) {
    df$score <- df[[1]] * w_education[i] + df[[2]] * w_occupation[i]
    set.seed(123)
    model <- as.formula(paste(columns[3], "~ score"))
    fit <- lm(model, data = df)
    rmse <- performance::rmse(fit, normalized = TRUE) ## !!
    w_df[i, ] <- c(w_education[i], w_occupation[i], rmse)
  }
  setNames(
    head(w_df[order(w_df$rmse), ]),
    c(
      "$w_{\\text{education}}$",
      "$w_{\\text{occupation}}$",
      "RMSE"
    )
  )
}
